## Summary

The Medieval Political Module adapts USTF to feudal, dynastic, religious, aristocratic, guild-based, and pre-modern political worlds. It models power through land, bloodline, oath, legitimacy, household, church, military obligation, slow communication, personal loyalty, custom, patronage, and scarcity.

This module is suitable for late medieval, early modern, fantasy-feudal, courtly intrigue, dynastic, merchant-republic, crusading, and religious-political campaigns.

---

## 1. Scope

Use when campaign power depends on personal bonds, land, custom, lineage, church, noble rank, dynastic inheritance, or pre-modern logistics.

Typical actions:

```text
Arrange marriage
Claim inheritance
Raise troops
Call vassals
Found institutions
Negotiate with church
Expand estates
Secure trade routes
Suppress rebellion
Manage court factions
Build fortifications
Send envoys
Conduct dynastic diplomacy
```

---

## 2. Core Assumptions

**MP-001:** Authority is personal, layered, and often ambiguous.

**MP-002:** Communication is slow and uncertain.

**MP-003:** Legitimacy matters as much as raw force.

**MP-004:** Oaths, customs, kinship, and patronage function as political infrastructure.

**MP-005:** Institutions exist, but bureaucratic capacity is limited compared to modern states.

**MP-006:** Rumor and reputation travel faster than verified knowledge.

## 6. Communication and Knowledge

This module modifies the practical availability of information but does not override Knowledge State.

Communication checks SHOULD consider:

```text
Distance
Road safety
Season
Messenger reliability
Literacy
Language
Political interception
Rumor distortion
Delay
```

A message sent is not automatically a message received.
A rumor known is not automatically a fact known.

---

## 7. Feudal Obligation Procedure

Calling on obligations SHOULD be procedural.

```text
1. Identify obligation source.
2. Check legal or customary basis.
3. Check relationship and loyalty.
4. Check Actor knowledge of summons.
5. Check available time and distance.
6. Check competing obligations.
7. Determine compliance, delay, refusal, bargaining, or betrayal.
8. Apply legitimacy and relationship consequences.
```

A vassal may comply reluctantly, delay strategically, negotiate terms, or claim lawful excuse.
